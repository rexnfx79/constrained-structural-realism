Epistemic Closure and Derivative Structure

By Alexander Seto

Rethinking Explanation After Gödel, Bell, and Holography

  • 30-minute read: Add Quantum information theory; Holography and black hole information; Cybernetics and embedded control.

Abstract

Across logic, mathematics, physics, information theory, and control, some of the most consequential modern results are not new mechanisms but boundary theorems: proof has limits (Gödel), prediction has limits (Turing), local descriptions have limits (Bell), and information extraction has limits (quantum information and gravitational bounds). These results are often treated as unrelated. This essay argues that they share a common structural source: embedded observers operate through compressed representations, and compressed internal access cannot stably reconstruct global structure. Constrained Structural Realism (CSR) preserves realism about structure while explaining why access to that structure is necessarily partial. It reframes “progress” as the discovery of invariants and constraints that survive theory change, rather than the expectation of a complete internal description.

Key claims

  • Structural realism, constrained. The world has objective structure, but an internal agent’s access to it is limited by measurement, resources, and self-reference.
  • Local is not global. Locally available observables, metrics, or rules often underdetermine global state; attempts to force completeness generate no-go results.
  • Compression is unavoidable. Any finite observer/controller must summarize; summaries are many-to-one projections that invite “boundary behavior” under recursion or optimization.
  • Limits are informative. No-go theorems, horizons, and instability regimes are not merely obstacles; they reveal invariant constraints on coherent description.
  • Domain-bridging hypothesis. Similar limit-shapes appear across fields because the same internal-access conditions recur, not because the fields reduce to one another.

Definitions and glossary

  • Embedded observer: an agent that is part of the system it studies or controls; observation and intervention are causally entangled.
  • Global structure: the full state/organization of a system (e.g., a global quantum state, a complete phase space distribution, or an institution’s full causal state).
  • Local access: the limited set of measurements, observables, or data streams available from within, often constrained by locality, resources, or horizons.
  • Compression / projection: any mapping from a high-dimensional state to a lower-dimensional representation (a proxy metric, an observable set, a model class). Many-to-one mappings lose information.
  • Proxy: a compressed indicator used to stand in for a richer target state (in science, measurement; in control, a cost function; in institutions, a metric).
  • Reflexivity: the condition where the representation affects the system it represents (measurement backreaction, incentive feedback, model-mediated behavior).
  • Structural Coupling: A term from cybernetics describing a history of recurrent interactions between two systems (e.g., observer and environment) that leads to structural congruence without direct information transfer.
  • Inference Device: A physical system (computer, brain) that processes information to predict or infer the state of another system. Wolpert proves that such devices cannot fully infer the state of a system containing them.
  • Boundary theorem / no-go result: a principled limit showing that a class of hoped-for descriptions or procedures cannot exist under stated assumptions.

Scope and interpretation note (read first)

This essay is not a new physical theory and does not offer new numerical predictions. It is a methodological and philosophical framework. References to “derivative structure,” “interfaces,” or “meta‑structure” are epistemic and explanatory claims: they concern what embedded observers can coherently measure, infer, and reconstruct from within a system. They are not direct assertions about what reality ultimately is “in itself.”

1. A puzzle about progress: why limits now dominate the frontier

For much of modern history, scientific progress looked like a steady march toward completeness: new observations led to new laws; new laws unified earlier laws; and unification suggested that a final, comprehensive picture might be attainable. That expectation still shapes how many people talk about science. We often assume that “understanding” means explaining everything in terms of deeper constituents.

Yet the last century introduced a different kind of “discovery”: not new entities, but new boundaries. Some of the most important advances did not expand our control so much as they clarified what cannot be done — or cannot be done in the way we hoped.

  • In mathematics and logic, we learned that there are true statements that cannot be proven within a given formal system (Gödel).
  • In computation, we learned that some program behaviors cannot be decided or predicted in general (Turing, Rice).
  • In quantum physics, we learned that no locally complete hidden‑variable story reproduces observed correlations (Bell) and that unknown quantum states cannot be copied (no‑cloning).
  • In gravity and cosmology, we encountered horizons, entropy bounds, and singularities — places where naive extrapolation breaks down.
  • In social systems, we learned that metrics collapse under optimization and incentives (Goodhart, Campbell), even when the metric “worked” before it became a target.

It would be convenient if these were just separate technical curiosities. But as the catalog of “no‑go” results grew, a question became difficult to ignore: why do limits with similar shapes keep appearing in domains that otherwise have little in common?

One plausible answer is simply: the world is complicated. Another is more structural: perhaps these limits recur because we keep encountering the same fundamental condition — we are attempting to know a system from within.

2. The recurring pattern: local rules, global ambition, principled failure

Across many domains, a familiar pattern repeats:

  1. The Ambition: We want a complete, consistent, and locally accessible description of the whole system (a complete axiom set, a universal predictor, a local hidden variable theory).
  2. The Constraint: The observer/describer is inside the system, meaning they have finite resources and must interact with what they describe.
  3. The Conflict: A self-referential or infinite-regress problem arises (e.g., "Does this set contain itself?", "Can I predict my own future output?", "Can I measure the state without disturbing it?").
  4. The Resolution (The Limit): The system preserves consistency by enforcing a limit on access. We get a theorem stating that the desired total description is impossible.

These limits are not failures of the scientist; they are features of the architecture. They protect the system from the paradoxes that would arise if a part could fully simulate the whole.

3. The embedded observer constraint

Classical physics implicitly assumed a "view from nowhere"—an observer who could measure the system without being part of its causal web. Relativity and Quantum Mechanics dismantled this.

An embedded observer:

  • Must use physical resources to measure: Measurement is an interaction, not a passive reading.
  • Has a horizon: Information takes time to travel; no observer sees the whole universe at once.
  • Is smaller than the whole: The observer cannot store the state of the entire universe without becoming the universe (and thus ceasing to be an observer).

This interaction is best described as structural coupling (a term from cybernetics): the observer and the system co-evolve. The observer does not just "read" the system; they are dynamically linked to it. This means that compression is not optional. All internal descriptions are lossy compressions. And lossy compressions, when pushed to the limit, generate artifacts—singularities, uncertainties, and undecidable propositions.

4. Russell and Gödel: totality and proof as early boundary discoveries

In the early 20th century, mathematicians sought to ground all of mathematics in a single, complete set of logical axioms (Hilbert's program).

Bertrand Russell showed that naive set theory allowed paradoxes (like the set of all sets that do not contain themselves). Kurt Gödel then proved that any sufficiently powerful formal system is either incomplete (there are true statements it cannot prove) or inconsistent (it proves false statements).

The Structural Lesson: You cannot formalize "everything" from the inside. A system cannot fully define its own truth conditions without generating contradictions.

5. Computation: prediction, decidability, and resource horizons

Alan Turing extended this to computation. The Halting Problem shows that no program can determine, for all possible programs, whether they will stop or run forever.

The Structural Lesson: Perfect prediction of a system that is as complex as the predictor is impossible. To predict the whole, you would need a simulation larger than the whole, which is impossible for an embedded part.

Recent work by David Wolpert on Inference Devices formalizes this. Wolpert proves that no physical inference device (computer or brain) contained within a universe can fully predict the state of that universe. There is always a "blind spot" created by the device's own processing state. This is not just a technological limit; it is a mathematical theorem about the limits of self-contained knowledge.

6. Bell, Nonlocality, and Relationality

John Bell showed that no theory based on "local hidden variables" (local instructions carried by particles) can explain the correlations observed in quantum mechanics. Bell-type inequalities follow from locality together with measurement independence and a hidden-variable completion; experiments violate these inequalities, forcing at least one of those assumptions to fail.

The Structural Lesson: The universe is not locally separable. The "state" is not just the sum of local states. An observer trying to build a picture of the world solely from local patches will necessarily miss the global correlations (entanglement).

This aligns with Relational Quantum Mechanics (RQM), which posits that quantum states are not absolute properties of a system but are relative to an observer. In RQM, there is no "view from nowhere"—every description is a description of a relation between two physical systems. This is the physical manifestation of the embedded observer constraint.

7. Quantum information theory: why degrees of freedom are not freely accessible

Quantum Information Theory (QIT) reveals that information is physical. The No-Cloning Theorem states you cannot make a perfect copy of an unknown quantum state.

The Structural Lesson: Information is not a free good. It is a conserved, physical resource. You cannot "read" the world without changing it, and you cannot duplicate it. This prevents the "view from nowhere" where an observer could passively accumulate infinite data.

8. Information, energy, and geometry: why “backreaction” is epistemically natural

In General Relativity, energy curves spacetime. To measure a system with arbitrary precision, you need high energy. If you concentrate enough energy in a small region to measure it perfectly, you create a black hole, hiding the very information you sought.

The Structural Lesson: The act of observation has a gravitational cost. There is a fundamental limit to how much information can be packed into a region (Bekenstein bound). The "hardware" of the universe (spacetime) collapses if you try to overload it with "software" (information).

9. Why constants are constant: invariants of coherent embedded description

Why are the speed of light (c) and Planck's constant (h) constant? In this framework, they are not just arbitrary numbers; they are the conversion factors of the interface.

  • c (Speed of Light): Defines the horizon of causality. It connects space and time.
  • h (Planck's Constant): Defines the granularity of action. It connects energy and frequency.

They represent the structural limits of the embedded observer's access to the world. They are the "resolution limits" of the screen we are looking at.

10. Are singularities real? Interface failure versus ontological infinity

Physics is full of singularities—points where quantities become infinite (Big Bang, Black Holes). A naive realist might say "the world contains infinities."

CSR suggests a different view: Singularities are interface failures. They are the points where our compressed description breaks down. They are not "things" in the world; they are the edges of our map. Just as a map of the Earth has a "singularity" at the poles (where longitude lines converge), our physical theories have singularities where our coordinate systems fail.

11. Goodhart and Reflexivity: incompleteness in social systems

"When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." (Goodhart's Law).

This is the social science equivalent of the observer effect. When an agent (policymaker) uses a compressed metric (GDP, test scores) to control a system they are part of, the system adapts to the metric, decoupling it from the reality it was meant to represent.

The Structural Lesson: You cannot control a complex adaptive system from the inside using a static, low-dimensional proxy. The system will always "outsmart" the metric because the system has more degrees of freedom than the controller.

12. Constrained Structural Realism: definition, commitments, and non‑commitments

"Reality is objective and structured, but embedded observers encounter principled limits on reconstructing global structure from local access."

CSR as a conditional claim

If observers are embedded, must act through compressed representations, and interventions feed back into the system, then boundary theorems are structurally expected. This turns the argument from "grand unification by analogy" into "shared mechanism under shared conditions."

Commitments

  • Realism about structure. There are mind-independent regularities and constraints.
  • Priority of invariants. What survives projection and perspective changes is the most robust object of knowledge.
  • Embedded access. Observation and control are physically and socially consequential interactions, not disembodied readings.

Non-commitments

  • CSR does not require a simulation hypothesis, multiverse, or design story.
  • CSR does not claim that all limits are the same theorem, only that they share a common structural shape. CSR claims a family resemblance driven by common ingredients (self-reference, projection, resource limits), not a single universal theorem with a uniform derivation.
  • CSR does not assert that a “final theory” is impossible in every sense; it challenges a specific expectation: that an internal theory can be globally complete and operationally accessible.

13. Implications: how CSR reframes research priorities and “success”

If CSR is right, we should stop looking for a "Theory of Everything" that explains every detail from a single axiom set. Instead, we should focus on:

  • Mapping the boundaries: Understanding the precise shape of the limits (like the Bekenstein bound) tells us more about the deep structure of reality than finding a new particle.
  • Meta-science: Studying how different observation scales create different "effective theories" (renormalization).
  • Resilient systems: In engineering and society, designing systems that are robust to our inevitable ignorance, rather than trying to eliminate it.

14. Objections, limits, and what would count against CSR

Objection: "This is just defeatism. We have always broken through limits before."

Response: We broke through technical limits (better telescopes), not logical limits. We didn't "break" Gödel's theorem; we accepted it. CSR argues that physical limits are more like Gödel's theorem than like a blurry lens.

What would falsify CSR? If we found a way to transmit information faster than light, or to copy quantum states, or to build a logical system that proves its own consistency, CSR would be wrong. The fact that these limits have held firm for a century suggests they are structural.

15. Closing: what this asks you to reconsider

We are used to thinking of knowledge as a light that expands into the dark. CSR suggests a different metaphor: knowledge is a structure we build within the dark. The walls we hit—the limits of logic, physics, and control—are not the end of the world. They are the touch of the real. They are the proof that we are not ghosts floating above the universe, but parts of it, woven into its fabric.
